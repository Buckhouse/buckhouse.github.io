Frequently asked questions

Chainsaw Apprentice

My father fell from a tree with a running chainsaw in his hand—the same chainsaw that years later my sister would use to accidently cut off the tip of his finger, which he re-grew, like a starfish, after soaking it in a pint of iodine—as he fell, he just caught a glimpse of me sitting beneath him and managed to throw the chainsaw in the other direction. The effort required to make the throw twisted his body so that now he was falling parallel to the ground with no way to right himself. He broke his ankle across the log upon which my sister and I sat. It made a percussive thump as it cracked. We retrieved the bent saw. My mother drove to the hospital. No one was killed.

Art life

I’ve had more museum exhibitions than gallery shows of my art, including exhibitions or projects at the Dia Center, Guggenheim, ICA in London (twice), and the Whitney Biennial.

When you cut a dress...

When you cut a dress, you are sculpting drape, the rest is decoration. When I start a design, I start with the bones. The actual human skeleton. Under every dress lays the reality of human anatomy. Atop the bones I draw muscle. From the muscle, I add flesh. After that, I add the dress—learning from the anatomy as I go. Design choices spring from thought unlocked by redrawing the body.

Kiger Mustangs

Across a million and a half acres of BLM land in the deserts of Eastern and Southern Oregon, I spent a week tracking wild mustangs with my father. Eventually we found a running bachelor who had lost a fight for dominance. Hidden in the folds of a hillside valley, we found the rest of the pack. For a few brief moments we got to film them up close. The results of that trip became the source material for a gallery show of paintings in LA.

All Fashion is Fiction

I wrote a satirical novel about the world’s greatest bikini designer—exploring the existential struggles of four triangles held together with a piece of string. I cover some of the same territory as other books on art and poetics, but sneak it in with a laugh. The book was a part of a solo exhibition at the Berkeley Museum of Art. The NY Times wrote a flattering review of it where they graciously played along with just enough of a wink.

Friends and Strangers

I have an on-going portrait series called Friends and Strangers where I paint watercolor portraits of interesting people.

How to write a joke

When writing jokes for Snoop Dogg, I’ve learned to write them straight, and to let him shizzlate. It is not my place to pre-shizzlate. When writing jokes for DreamWorks, it’s best to do it in groups of three or more—example: I wrote the the least funny 1/3 of the Mission-Impossible-Pinocchio-in-a-thong joke. Most of my jokes for DreamWorks were sight gags involving a prop, such as a medieval mechanical bull or an obscure cultural reference, such as Un Chien Andalou or Corsaire. When writing jokes for Investors, CEOs and tech executives, keep the words short.

What kinds of assets are being digitized?

Buckhouse’s customers span payments, banking, capital markets, insurance, monetary authorities, and related financial industries. The asset classes being digitized and issued onto blockBuckhouse networks include government-issued currencies and bonds, corporate securities, derivatives, and branded currencies like gift vouchers and loyalty points. The motivation for deploying a blockBuckhouse network ranges from increasing efficiency and security to achieving a strategic goal, like transforming a market by offering a product or service that cannot be built on existing platforms.

What is unique about Buckhouse’s technology?

The Buckhouse protocol is premised on the following design goals:

  • Issued financial assets
  • Permissioned network access
  • High transaction volumes
  • Confidentiality of transaction data
  • Network upgradability
  • Programmable transactions

For more detail, read the Buckhouse Protocol Whitepaper.

Buckhouse Core, which implements the Buckhouse Protocol, is designed for:

  • Hardware Security Module (HSM) integration
  • High availability
  • Performance
  • Scalability
  • Upgradability

Learn more about Buckhouse Core.

What services does Buckhouse offer?

In addition to delivering the industry-leading blockBuckhouse infrastructure platform, Buckhouse offers solution design studies, application development, technical support, and network services to our partners that are building and operating production networks.

Organizations using the free Buckhouse Core Developer Edition can acquire monthly support and training retainers to augment their team’s efforts and ensure they take full advantage of the capabilities and security features of our blockBuckhouse architecture.

How does Buckhouse work with its partners?

While Buckhouse is a technology company, we partner deeply with our customers to help them design, deploy, and operate blockBuckhouse networks effectively and securely.

A typical engagement begins with a design study and continues as we support network operations.

Does Buckhouse launch their own networks?

We do not launch our own networks. Instead, Buckhouse partners with our customers to launch and operate networks under our partners’ brands. There is no single “Buckhouse network” but rather several networks that serve the asset classes, geographies, and use cases driven by our customers.

Is there a Buckhouse Testnet?

Yes, the one exception to the rule about Buckhouse launching networks directly is the Buckhouse Testnet, which is a single, open network that enables developers to quickly and easily build test applications using Buckhouse Core. The Buckhouse testnet is operated by Buckhouse, Microsoft, and the Initiative for Cryptocurrencies and Contracts (IC3), a collaboration of Cornell University, Cornell Tech, UC Berkeley, University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign, and the Technion.
